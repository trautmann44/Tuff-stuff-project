# From Test to Task: Relevance of Basic Physical Fitness Metrics in the Context of Military-Specific Performance

## Overview

This project is based on a published study examining the relationship between traditional physical fitness assessments and performance
in military-specific tasks. While standard tests such as push-ups, sit-ups, and the 12-minute run are widely used to evaluate military 
readiness, their ability to explain real-world occupational performance remains limited.

The primary aim of this analysis is to investigate how well conventional fitness metrics predict performance in simulated military 
tasks performed under standardized load conditions.

## Study Design

Twenty-nine physically active men (mean age = 22.6 ± 3.0 years) completed a comprehensive battery of physical fitness assessments, including:

- Maximal oxygen uptake (V̇O₂max)
- Body composition analysis
- Aerobic endurance (12-minute Cooper test)
- Anaerobic agility (shuttle run)
- Muscular endurance (push-ups, sit-ups, pull-ups)
- Explosive power (countermovement jump, seated medicine ball throw)
- Grip strength
- Maximal strength (1-repetition maximum hexagonal barbell deadlift)

## Military-Specific Performance Tasks

Participants performed six standardized military tasks while carrying 10.4 kg combat gear:

- 2 km loaded march  
- Casualty drag  
- Maximal single lift of a military pack  
- Repeated lift and carry  
- Modified fire and movement  
- Water can carry  

## Key Findings

- The **12-minute Cooper run** emerged as the most consistent predictor of performance, particularly for:
  - 2 km loaded march (β = −0.233)
  - Repeated lift and carry (β = −0.062)

- Strength and power measures were **task-specific rather than general predictors**:
  - Seated medicine ball throw and grip strength were associated with maximal lifting performance
  - Shuttle run performance aligned with modified fire and movement efficiency

## Conclusion

The results indicate that traditional physical fitness tests provide only limited explanatory value for military-specific 
performance outcomes beyond aerobic endurance (Cooper test). Military-specific task simulations may therefore represent a more
valid measure of occupational readiness than conventional fitness assessments alone.
